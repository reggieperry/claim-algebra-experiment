A claim algebra for agent networks — Belnap-completed

Revision 2. The verifiable inter-agent message as a bilattice-graded object — Belnap's four values realized over the free provenance semiring, with gap and glut as distinct values, negation first-class, and verification kept as a third axis.
Working note · revises the single-channel semiring of Rev 1 · companion to the focused-harness actor-model design and the Credit & Loan Agreement Workbench · math via MathJax, diagrams via Mermaid + SVG

A network of LLM agents is only as trustworthy as the messages they pass each other. Today those messages are unverifiable prose, so a confident error at one hop propagates with full authority to the next — and, just as corrosively, a contradiction between two upstream agents arrives looking exactly like an honest absence of information. This note proposes the fix as a single mathematical object — a claim that carries its own provenance, a computed confidence, a fail-closed semantics, and now a faithful distinction between not knowing and being told both — and argues that the object is best understood as a three-level tower: a categorical abstraction, realized by concrete algebraic structures, implemented as a programming type.

The one-paragraph thesis (revised). Make every inter-agent message a value annotated by an element of a distributive bilattice — Belnap's FOUR, graded — realized as two evidential channels (support-for and support-against) over the free provenance semiring. UNRESOLVED is no longer the lone zero; it is the knowledge-bottom — the gap, Belnap's \(\mathbf{N}\) — and a new value GLUT (Belnap's \(\mathbf{B}\)) is the knowledge-top, reached when support and refutation meet. Fail-closed is still an annihilator law, now named: it is the knowledge-meet's annihilator \(\mathbf{N}\otimes_k x = \mathbf{N}\). Pluggable trust models are still homomorphisms — now one per channel, via the twist representation \(\mathsf{FOUR}\cong\mathsf{TWO}\odot\mathsf{TWO}\), evaluating a pro-polynomial and a con-polynomial separately. Negation becomes first-class. The message is a bilattice-graded applicative refined by a decidable verification predicate — and verification remains a third axis, not Belnap's second. The mathematics (Belnap 1977; Dunn; Ginsberg–Fitting bilattices; Green–Karvounarakis–Tannen provenance; subjective logic) is decades old; using a graded bilattice as the typed wire format between LLM agents is the new part.
What changed in this revision rev 2 Rev 1 annotated messages with a one-channel positive commutative semiring whose single bottom, UNRESOLVED, conflated "no information" with "contradiction" — the limitation §9 flagged as no conflict or non-monotonic retraction. 1 · The idea

Picture three agents. One extracts a borrower's total debt from a filing; one extracts its EBITDA; a third divides them to report leverage. In a prose pipeline, the third agent receives "debt is $1.2bn" and "EBITDA is $400m" as sentences, believes them, and emits "leverage is 3.0x" as another sentence. Nothing in the message records where $1.2bn came from, how sure the extractor was, or what should happen if the EBITDA agent actually failed and guessed. An error at hop one reaches the signed memo at hop three wearing the same confident clothes as a fact.

The remedy is to stop passing values and start passing claims. A claim bundles the things below, and the rest of this note is about giving them a precise mathematical home.

The example Rev 1 could not express. Now add a fourth agent: a second debt extractor that reads $1.5bn from a different exhibit. The network now holds two cited, mutually contradictory debt figures. In a prose pipeline one silently wins. In Rev 1's algebra, "$1.2bn cited AND $1.5bn cited" collapses to UNRESOLVED — the very same value as "no debt figure was ever found." That is the bug: ignorance and contradiction are not the same epistemic state, and an agent that cannot tell them apart cannot act correctly — one calls for a search, the other for reconciliation or escalation. Belnap's logic exists precisely to separate them: the gap \(\mathbf{N}\) (told nothing) from the glut \(\mathbf{B}\) (told both). This revision makes that distinction native, so the fourth agent emits a GLUT, not an UNRESOLVED.

A claim, completed, bundles seven things:

#Feature of a claimInformal meaning
1Value with two-channel support — yielding True / False / Unknown / ConflictedA payload carried with independent evidence-for and evidence-against. The four combinations are Belnap's values: support only → True; refutation only → False; neither → Unknown (the gap, the old UNRESOLVED); both → Conflicted (the glut, new).
2Provenance, now two-channelWhere support came from and where refutation came from — source spans, tool outputs, and for derived claims the input claims each was computed from (its lineage). A pro-lineage and a con-lineage.
3ConfidenceA graded signal computed from the derivation, never asserted by the producer. Graded now means a pair: degree-for and degree-against.
4Fail-closedCombine anything conjunctively with an UNRESOLVED input, under the knowledge-meet, and the result is UNRESOLVED. Missing support cannot be laundered into a confident answer.
5Gap ≠ glut new"No one told me" and "I was told contradictory things" are distinct values that propagate differently and license different downstream actions.
6Negation & retraction newA claim can be refuted, not merely unsupported; negation swaps the two channels. A later source that supersedes an earlier one is a retraction, expressible as refutation of the superseded support.
7Composition & verificationConjunctive ("derived from A and B") and disjunctive ("corroborated by A or B"); and the value is checkable against its provenance — a predicate separate from how-confident-given-the-derivation, and separate again from the for/against evidential content.

2 · The tower: abstraction, realization, implementation

The natural question — and the one that prompted Rev 1 — is whether the right description is category-theoretic. The answer is yes, with discipline: category theory belongs at the top, where it captures the abstraction several concrete structures share, and it should be used only where it earns its keep. Below it sit the algebraic structures that realize the abstraction, and below those, the type that implements them. The revision changes the middle floor — a semiring becomes a bilattice — and, because a bilattice is two interlaced lattices rather than one, it changes how the top floor pays for itself.

The honesty rule for the top level (revised). Category theory earns its place here in exactly three ways now: (i) the universal property of the free semiring — applied per channel — which keeps "compute provenance once, evaluate many trust models" a one-line theorem; (ii) preservation of the zeros by morphisms, which makes fail-closed hold under every trust model at once; and (iii) the twist (product) representation of distributive bilattices, \(\mathsf{FOUR}\cong\mathsf{TWO}\odot\mathsf{TWO}\), which is what lets (i) and (ii) survive the upgrade from a semiring to a bilattice — it says a bilattice is just a pair of lattice computations bolted together, so the semiring machinery applies twice rather than not at all. Past these three, further categorical generality would be decoration, and the note drops to the algebra. Abstraction is a tool for compression, not a flex.

Two layers, two names (unchanged in spirit). The general object at the algebraic and type levels — a value (or UNRESOLVED) carried with its provenance and a grade in the value space \(K\) — is an Axia (Greek axía, "worth"). It is value-agnostic: the payload may be a fact, a cost, or a trust level, and the entire algebra of §3 and §4 operates at this layer. A Claim is an Axia refined by a verification predicate — an Axia that asserts something and can be checked against its provenance. The one thing the revision sharpens here is what \(K\) is: in Rev 1 it was a commutative semiring; now it is a distributive bilattice, and an Axia therefore carries a Belnap-valued (graded) annotation rather than a single semiring grade. The cost-routing instance (§7) is still naturally an Axia, a graded cost; the workbench's cited facts and Diplomacy's promises are Claims, because there the verification — and now the for/against bookkeeping — is the point.

3 · The categorical layer abstraction

3.1 · Claims compose — so they live in a monoidal category

Agents take claims in and put claims out; they are morphisms. Two claims can be combined into one (the leverage example). The structure that captures "objects, morphisms, and a way to combine things side by side" is a symmetric monoidal category \((\mathcal{C}, \otimes, I)\): the tensor \(\otimes\) is conjunctive composition, and the unit \(I\) is the trivially-true claim. An agent network is then a string diagram in \(\mathcal{C}\) — boxes are agents, wires carry claims, side-by-side wires are conjunction, and the diagram's value is read by composing the boxes [5, 6, 12].

One subtlety the revision forces here. Once values can be refuted, "conjunction" is no longer a single operation — there are two monoidal products sharing the same objects, the knowledge-tensor and the truth-tensor (§4.3). The string diagram is unchanged as a picture; what changes is that each box must say which tensor its fan-in uses. Fail-closed pins the default to the knowledge-tensor.

3.2 · The trust annotation is a lax monoidal functor

A claim is a value decorated with provenance and confidence. The decoration is a functor \(F\) that, crucially, knows how to combine decorations when values combine. The precise statement is that \(F\) is a lax monoidal functor: there is a natural map $$ F a \;\otimes\; F b \;\longrightarrow\; F(a \otimes b), $$ which says "given a decorated \(a\) and a decorated \(b\), produce a decorated \(a \otimes b\)" — combine the two annotations into the annotation of the combination. This is not a coincidence of vocabulary: applicative functors are exactly lax monoidal functors [2, 11]. The revision's only change is that the decoration is now a pair of channels, so the lax map combines pro with pro and con with con; §5's type writes this out in a language with a compiler.

3.3 · The universal property — compute provenance once, evaluate many trust models rev

Here is where category theory paid for itself in Rev 1, and where the upgrade to a bilattice could most easily have broken it. Recall the Rev-1 statement. Let \(X\) be the set of leaf sources. The provenance of a derived claim is a polynomial in the \(X\): a sum (alternative derivations) of products (conjoined supports). These polynomials form the free commutative semiring \(\mathbb{N}[X]\) — the free object on \(X\) in commutative semirings. Freeness is a universal property: for any commutative semiring \(K\) and any valuation \(\nu : X \to K\), there is a unique semiring homomorphism \(\hat\nu : \mathbb{N}[X] \to K\) with \(\hat\nu \circ \iota = \nu\). You compute the lineage polynomial once and every trust measure is a homomorphic evaluation of it — the Green–Karvounarakis–Tannen theorem [1].

The obstacle. The GKT theorem is about a semiring. A bilattice is not a semiring — it is two lattices interlaced (two semirings' worth of structure, plus a negation tying them). A naive reader fears the crown jewel is lost: there is no single free bilattice homomorphism that the GKT result hands you.

The rescue is the twist. A distributive bilattice is, up to isomorphism, a product of two copies of an underlying lattice with a swap: \(\mathsf{FOUR}\cong\mathsf{TWO}\odot\mathsf{TWO}\), and more generally \(K\cong L\odot L\) for a lattice \(L\) [Fitting]. Read an element as a pair \((a^{+},a^{-})\) — degree-for and degree-against. Then a derived claim carries two independent lineage polynomials, a pro-polynomial \(p^{+}\in\mathbb{N}[X]\) and a con-polynomial \(p^{-}\in\mathbb{N}[X]\); each is evaluated by its own GKT homomorphism into the underlying lattice \(L\); and the bilattice value is read off the resulting pair. So the universal property is not lost — it applies twice, once per channel. "Compute once, evaluate many" becomes "compute two lineages once, evaluate each under one homomorphism," and a glut is simply the case where both evaluated channels are non-bottom.

Why fail-closed is still free — and now precisely which zero. A bilattice has two bottoms: the knowledge-bottom \(\mathbf{N}\) (the gap) and the truth-bottom \(\mathbf{F}\) (refuted). Every lattice homomorphism on each channel preserves its bottom, so both survive evaluation into every trust model. Fail-closed is the law of the knowledge-meet: a conjunctive derivation with a missing (gap) input lands on \(\mathbf{N}\), under every \(\hat h\) — exactly the Rev-1 behavior, now stated as a choice of which annihilator. A refuted input, by contrast, drives the truth-meet to \(\mathbf{F}\): the result is false, not unknown. The two outcomes are different, and §4.3 makes the fork explicit.

4 · The algebraic layer realization rev

4.1 · The object: a distributive bilattice over an absorbing-zero lattice

Rev 1's object was a commutative semiring \((K,\oplus,\otimes,0,1)\) whose boxed law \(0\otimes x=0\) carried the whole safety story. The revision keeps that law and adds the missing structure around it. A distributive bilattice \((K,\le_t,\le_k)\) is a set carrying two lattice orderings at once — a truth order \(\le_t\) and a knowledge (information) order \(\le_k\) — that interlace (each lattice operation is monotone with respect to the other order), together with a negation \(\lnot\) that inverts \(\le_t\) and fixes \(\le_k\). The smallest non-trivial one is Belnap's \(\mathsf{FOUR}\) [7].

The clean way to hold it is the twist: \(K\cong L\odot L\), an element being a pair \((a^{+},a^{-})\) of degree-for and degree-against drawn from an underlying lattice \(L\). All four operations are then componentwise min/max, and negation is the swap:

OperationOn pairs \((a^{+},a^{-})\)IdentityAnnihilatorReading
truth-meet \(\wedge_t\)\((\min a^{+},\ \max a^{-})\)\(\mathbf{T}=(\top,\bot)\)\(\mathbf{F}=(\bot,\top)\)conservative AND on truth: refutation wins
truth-join \(\vee_t\)\((\max a^{+},\ \min a^{-})\)\(\mathbf{F}\)\(\mathbf{T}\)optimistic OR on truth: support wins
knowledge-meet \(\otimes_k\)\((\min a^{+},\ \min a^{-})\)\(\mathbf{B}=(\top,\top)\)\(\mathbf{N}=(\bot,\bot)\)fail-closed: a gap input erases evidence
knowledge-join \(\oplus_k\)\((\max a^{+},\ \max a^{-})\)\(\mathbf{N}\)\(\mathbf{B}\)corroboration: pool evidence; conflict → glut
negation \(\lnot\)\((a^{-},\ a^{+})\)involution: \(\lnot\lnot=\mathrm{id}\)swap for and against
The one-line reconciliation with Rev 1. Rev 1's conjunction \(\otimes\) had annihilator UNRESOLVED and Rev 1's corroboration \(\oplus\) had identity UNRESOLVED. In the table those are exactly \(\otimes_k\) and \(\oplus_k\) — the knowledge-lattice meet and join, with \(\mathbf{N}=\) UNRESOLVED. So Rev 1 was computing in the knowledge lattice the whole time; it simply never reached the top \(\mathbf{B}\), because with only a "for" channel nothing could ever push a value to \((\,\cdot\,,\top)\). The revision adds the "against" channel, and the rest of the bilattice — the value \(\mathbf{F}\), the value \(\mathbf{B}\), the truth lattice, negation — appears for free.

4.2 · The universal instance and the concrete instances

The universal \(K\) is now the twist over the provenance polynomial semiring, \(\mathbb{N}[X]\odot\mathbb{N}[X]\) — you compute the two lineages in it once (§3.3). The concrete \(K\) you evaluate into is a design choice. The smallest interesting one is \(\mathsf{FOUR}\) itself — the deterministic True / False / Unknown / Conflicted signal. The graded one is \([0,1]^2\): a degree-for and a degree-against, which is precisely subjective logic [8] (belief = for, disbelief = against, with uncertainty mass measuring gap-ness and conflict mass measuring glut-ness) and, equivalently, the Dempster–Shafer reading [13]. Rev 1's standard evaluation semirings (Viterbi, fuzzy, tropical, Boolean) survive as the per-channel \(L\) you twist.

Where the old confidence lattice went. Rev 1's headline instance was the chain \(\{\bot<\mathrm{Low}<\mathrm{Med}<\mathrm{High}\}\), "a four-element idempotent semiring." It is not gone — it is now the underlying lattice \(L\) of the twist, supplying the graded truth axis. The four-element chain and the four-element bilattice \(\mathsf{FOUR}\) are different objects of the same size: the chain is totally ordered (one axis), \(\mathsf{FOUR}\) is the two-axis diamond of Figure 4. Conflating them by cardinality was the loose move §4.4 cleans up.

4.3 · Fail-closed semantics — now a fork, deliberately resolved

"Fail-closed" is the principle from security engineering: when a result is missing, unverifiable, or uncertain, default to the safe, restrictive state — no usable value — rather than proceeding with a guess. A lock that fails closed stays locked when the power dies; default-deny authorization is fail-closed. Its mistakes are over-refusals (a loss of throughput, not of correctness); the opposite, fail-open, lets a wrong value through with authority (a loss of correctness). Flipping most language-model systems' fail-open default — when uncertain, emit a fluent guess — is the point.

In Rev 1 there was one conjunction, so one annihilator, so one fail-closed law. With \(\mathbf{F}\) and \(\mathbf{B}\) present there are two meets, and they disagree on what a bad input does:

Inputsknowledge-meet \(\otimes_k\)truth-meet \(\wedge_t\)Gloss
\(\mathbf{T}\) and \(\mathbf{N}\) (a gap)\(\mathbf{N}\)\(\mathbf{N}\)missing support ⇒ unknown either way (both meets agree)
\(\mathbf{T}\) and \(\mathbf{F}\) (refuted)\(\mathbf{N}\) — "we now know nothing"\(\mathbf{F}\) — "the result is false"they diverge: this is the choice the bilattice exposes

The derivation \(\mathbf{T}\otimes_k\mathbf{F}=(\top,\bot)\otimes_k(\bot,\top)=(\bot,\bot)=\mathbf{N}\), whereas \(\mathbf{T}\wedge_t\mathbf{F}=(\top,\bot)\wedge_t(\bot,\top)=(\bot,\top)=\mathbf{F}\). Rev 1's fail-closed-to-UNRESOLVED is the knowledge-meet, and the revision keeps that as the default for derivation, so missing support still propagates closed to \(\mathbf{N}\). What is new is that a genuine refutation of a conjunct can now be expressed as \(\mathbf{F}\) under the truth-meet — information Rev 1 had no value for. The bilattice does not pick the meet for you; the engineering decision is to declare the knowledge-meet for "missing support" and reserve the truth-meet for "actively refuted."

4.4 · The evidential pair is Belnap's second axis — verification is a third

This subsection replaces Rev 1's "verification is a second, orthogonal axis," which invoked Belnap loosely and, as the working notes that led to this revision pointed out, mislabeled the axes. Here is the faithful picture.

Confidence answers "how strong is the derivation, assuming the inputs hold?" — and that grading now lives on the truth axis of Figure 4 (the \(L\) of the twist). The evidential content — supported, refuted, neither, both — is the for/against pair, and that pair is exactly Belnap's structure: the knowledge order runs gap \(\mathbf{N}\) → glut \(\mathbf{B}\), the truth order runs \(\mathbf{F}\) → \(\mathbf{T}\). Both orderings are internal to the evidential pair. Verification answers a different question again: "does the value actually reproduce against the provenance it cites?" — re-resolve the citation, re-match the quote. This is a decidable predicate \(\mathrm{verify} : \mathrm{Claim}\,a \to \mathrm{Bool}\), and it is not one of Belnap's two orderings. It is a third axis.

The correction, stated plainly. Rev 1 drew a 2×2 grid with confidence on one axis and verification on the other and called it "a bilattice in the sense of Belnap." But confidence and verification are not Belnap's truth and knowledge orders. Belnap's two orders are truth (false→true) and knowledge (gap→glut), and both are evidential. So a faithful object tracks three things, not two: support-for, support-against (these two are the Belnap value), and verified? (the orthogonal refinement). The thing Rev 1 labelled "high confidence · UNVERIFIED" is a point on the verification axis crossed with a high truth-grade — it is not the glut \(\mathbf{B}\), and never was. The glut is "supported and refuted," a different idea entirely.

The acceptance policy that gates a signature is then the conjunction of all three: the value must be True (not gap, not glut, not false), its derivation grade must clear a threshold, and it must verify: $$ \mathrm{accept}(c)\iff \mathrm{value}(c)=\mathbf{T}\ \wedge\ \mathrm{grade}(c)\ge\theta\ \wedge\ \mathrm{verify}(c). $$

5 · The type layer code rev

The categorical statement of §3.2 — applicative = lax monoidal functor — is still a programming type; the revision widens its grade from a single semiring value to a bilattice element, i.e. a pair of channels. The two names of §2 are unchanged: Axia[K, A] is the graded value the algebra acts on, Claim[K, A] is an Axia refined by verification. Here it is in Scala, lightly cleaned.

// The underlying lattice L of the twist (Rev 1's confidence lattice, now one channel).
sealed trait Lev
case object Bot extends Lev                          // ⊥  (no evidence on this channel)
final case class Deg(p: Double) extends Lev          // p in (0,1]; Deg(1.0) = ⊤ ("certain")
object Lev:
  def meet(a: Lev, b: Lev): Lev = (a, b) match { case (Bot,_)|(_,Bot)=>Bot
    case (Deg(x),Deg(y))=>Deg(x min y) }             // min
  def join(a: Lev, b: Lev): Lev = (a, b) match { case (Bot,y)=>y; case (x,Bot)=>x
    case (Deg(x),Deg(y))=>Deg(x max y) }             // max

// THE BILATTICE ELEMENT: an evidential PAIR (for, against) — the twist L ⊙ L.
final case class Ev(pro: Lev, con: Lev)              // (a⁺, a⁻)
object Ev:
  val N = Ev(Bot, Bot)                               // gap   — knowledge-bottom, the old UNRESOLVED
  val T = Ev(Deg(1.0), Bot)                          // true  — supported, unrefuted
  val F = Ev(Bot, Deg(1.0))                          // false — refuted, unsupported
  val B = Ev(Deg(1.0), Deg(1.0))                     // glut  — knowledge-top, told both

  // FOUR OPERATIONS, all componentwise (see §4.1 table):
  def kmeet(a: Ev, b: Ev) = Ev(Lev.meet(a.pro,b.pro), Lev.meet(a.con,b.con)) // ⊗ₖ FAIL-CLOSED: N absorbs
  def kjoin(a: Ev, b: Ev) = Ev(Lev.join(a.pro,b.pro), Lev.join(a.con,b.con)) // ⊕ₖ corroborate; conflict → B
  def tmeet(a: Ev, b: Ev) = Ev(Lev.meet(a.pro,b.pro), Lev.join(a.con,b.con)) // ∧ₜ truth-AND; F absorbs
  def tjoin(a: Ev, b: Ev) = Ev(Lev.join(a.pro,b.pro), Lev.meet(a.con,b.con)) // ∨ₜ truth-OR
  def negate(a: Ev): Ev   = Ev(a.con, a.pro)                                 // ¬ swap channels

  enum Belnap { case Gap, True_, False_, Glut }
  def corner(a: Ev): Belnap = (a.pro, a.con) match    // read the Belnap value off the pair
    case (Bot, Bot) => Belnap.Gap
    case (_,   Bot) => Belnap.True_
    case (Bot, _  ) => Belnap.False_
    case (_,   _  ) => Belnap.Glut                    // both channels fire — the value Rev 1 lacked

// Provenance is now TWO free commutative monoids — a pro-lineage and a con-lineage.
final case class Prov(atoms: Set[Lineage])
object Prov:
  given Monoid[Prov] = Monoid.instance(Prov(Set.empty), (a,b) => Prov(a.atoms | b.atoms))

// THE AXIA: a value carried with (pro-prov, con-prov, evidential grade). Value-agnostic.
final case class Axia[+A](value: Option[A], provPro: Prov, provCon: Prov, ev: Ev)

object Axia:
  // CONJUNCTIVE — the Validation applicative: accumulates BOTH lineage channels, never short-circuits.
  def and2[A, B, C](a: Axia[A], b: Axia[B])(f: (A, B) => C): Axia[C] =
    val e = Ev.kmeet(a.ev, b.ev)                      // fail-closed under ⊗ₖ  (§4.3 default)
    val v = if e.pro == Bot then None else (a.value, b.value).mapN(f)  // no support ⇒ no value
    Axia(v, Prov(a.provPro.atoms | b.provPro.atoms),
            Prov(a.provCon.atoms | b.provCon.atoms), e)

  // DISJUNCTIVE — corroboration. Agreeing sources reinforce; DISAGREEING sources climb to GLUT.
  def corroborate[A](a: Axia[A], b: Axia[A]): Axia[A] =
    Axia(a.value.orElse(b.value),
         Prov(a.provPro.atoms | b.provPro.atoms),
         Prov(a.provCon.atoms | b.provCon.atoms), Ev.kjoin(a.ev, b.ev))

  // NEGATION / RETRACTION — swap the channels (a superseding source refutes prior support).
  def refute[A](a: Axia[A]): Axia[A] = Axia(a.value, a.provCon, a.provPro, Ev.negate(a.ev))

// A CLAIM is an Axia refined by verification — unchanged in role, now over a bilattice grade.
type Claim[A] = Axia[A] Refined VerifiesAgainstProvenance   // verify(c) recomputes value vs provPro
Why an applicative, not a monad (unchanged, now doubled). Monadic >>= short-circuits on the first failure, discarding the lineage of the other conjuncts; the Validation applicative is deliberately not a monad so it accumulates [2]. The revision only raises the stakes: it must accumulate both the pro- and con-lineages of every conjunct, because a downstream agent needs the full two-channel provenance for verification, blame-assignment, and to tell a glut from a gap. The single legitimately-monadic edge — an agent choosing whom to ask next from a prior answer — remains a justified escape hatch.

The other realizations move in step. In Haskell the object is Axia a = Writer (Prov, Prov, Ev) (Maybe a) exposed as an Applicative only — the monoid is now the product of the two provenance monoids and the bilattice grade — with Claim via a Liquid Haskell refinement, and negate a total function swapping the two Prov components. In the harness's Go (no higher-kinded types) the Axia struct simply grows a second provenance field and the Conf enum becomes the four-corner Ev; And still pattern-matches the gap first (now Ev.N) as an explicit early return, but the glut is finally representable as a value rather than silently folded into the gap.

7 · A second instance: cost routing in an agent fulfillment network

§6 read the workbench as the \(N=1\) specialization. The same object at a different value space is a network of fulfillment-center agents finding the cheapest way to move an item to a customer — the cost sibling. It exercises the same core moves on a problem that looks nothing like clause extraction, and the revision touches it in one specific place: the confidence riding on an advertised cost becomes Belnap-valued, while the cost dimension itself stays one-dimensional.

7.1 · The tropical specialization (unchanged)

Take the cost channel to be the tropical (min-plus) semiring \((\mathbb{R}\cup\{+\infty\},\ \min,\ +,\ +\infty,\ 0)\). Nodes are fulfillment centers; edges are lanes; an edge's weight is its cost. Conjunction \(+\) adds cost along a path; disjunction \(\min\) picks the cheapest alternative. The tropical zero \(+\infty\) plays a dual role: as the \(\min\)-identity it is "no route found yet," and as the \(+\)-annihilator it is "this leg is infeasible" (\(+\infty + x = +\infty\)). Optimal routing is the Kleene closure of the tropical adjacency matrix — Bellman–Ford and Floyd–Warshall are this computation [14, 15], converging when there is no negative-cost cycle.

What the revision does and does not change here. The cost itself is a scalar in a totally ordered semiring; there is no "for/against" on a number, and the note does not bilattice-ify cost. What becomes Belnap-valued is the confidence a center attaches to its advertised cost: a lane can now be actively refuted ("a probe reports this lane is down") — value \(\mathbf{F}\) — which is distinct from "no probe yet" — value \(\mathbf{N}\) — and from two probes disagreeing — value \(\mathbf{B}\). So the cost stays tropical; the evidential pair rides alongside it.

7.2 · The message one center sends another rev

A center \(B\) advertises to a neighbor \(A\), per destination, a cost-to-serve claim — a path-vector advertisement. The revision splits the single confidence field into the evidential pair:

final case class ReachClaim(
  dest:     DestId,                                  // the QUESTION: which customer / region
  cost:     Cost | Unreachable,                      // tropical VALUE (Unreachable = +inf)
  route:    Vector[Leg],                             // PROVENANCE — an ORDERED path witness (free monoid)
  ev:       Ev,                                      // EVIDENTIAL pair on the advertised cost (rev):
                                                     //   True = probed & holds · False = probed down
                                                     //   Gap = unprobed · Glut = probes disagree
  verifier: EvidenceRef,                             // VERIFICATION handle — re-check the lane vs evidence
  asOf:     Instant, ttl: Duration,                  // freshness (a min-lattice)
  basis:    Indicative | Reservable(CapacityToken))  // estimate vs committable hold (Boolean-AND)

\(A\) relaxes by composing its lane to \(B\) with \(B\)'s claim: cost is tropical \(+\) (with \(+\infty\) absorbing); route is \((A\!\to\!B)\) prepended; the evidential pair is the knowledge-meet \(\otimes_k\) of the two legs' pairs (a refuted leg makes the composed evidence false, an unprobed leg makes it a gap); freshness is the \(\min\); and basis is reservable only if both legs are. \(A\)'s own advertised cost is the \(\min\) over neighbors, carrying the arg-min's label. The verifier field is still the §4.4 third axis: an advertised cost is a Claim[Cost] whose verify() re-resolves the lane against the agent's evidence, so a high-grade-but-unverified quote sits in the danger cell of Figure 6 — and, separately, a glut now flags two rate cards that disagree.

7.3 · Provenance buys loop-freedom (unchanged)

Carrying the full ordered route lineage, and discarding any advertisement whose route already contains the receiver, makes this a path-vector protocol — the AS_PATH loop check of BGP [17] — inherently loop-free, with no count-to-infinity, unlike bare distance-vector RIP [18]. An Unreachable advertisement (\(+\infty\)) is an active route withdrawal. None of this is touched by the bilattice upgrade, because loop-freedom rides on the order of the provenance witness, not on the evidential pair.

What this still does not give you for free. "Compute the route once, then re-optimize for cost, time, or carbon by swapping the homomorphism" remains false: changing the objective changes the chosen lane, and shortest-path selection is a min-fold under one cost valuation, so collapsing to the cost-min discards the alternatives. True multi-objective routing needs a lexicographic or Pareto/antichain semiring [19], the latter computing the frontier once but at NP-hard cost. The bilattice upgrade does not change this; it concerns the confidence channel, not the cost objective.

8 · A third instance: adversarial games (Diplomacy) rev

The cost network is cooperative — centers have no incentive to lie about lane costs. The sharper test is an adversarial setting where agents do have an incentive to deceive, and Diplomacy is the canonical one: seven powers, simultaneous moves, binding-only-if-kept promises, and a board whose state is unambiguous. It splits cleanly into a part the algebra governs and a part it does not — and the revision moves the boundary, because the trust model Rev 1 said "leaves the clean semiring" is, once the algebra is a graded bilattice, no longer outside it.

8.1 · The board substrate — the consistent fragment of Belnap

Board state is the cooperative substrate, and it is governed by the algebra exactly as before. Unit positions, supply-center ownership, and the legality and resolution of orders are objective facts the adjudicator computes; an order claim verifies against the rules, and an illegal order is refuted and reduced to a Hold (an annihilator-to-default move). One observation the revision adds: because the game engine is an oracle, board facts can never be contradictory — there is no way to be told two conflicting truths about whether Paris holds an army. So board-state claims live in the consistent fragment of Belnap: they range over \(\{\mathbf{T},\mathbf{N}\}\) (and \(\mathbf{F}\) for a refuted order), but never reach the glut \(\mathbf{B}\). The substrate is the gap-free, glut-free corner of the bilattice — which is exactly why Rev 1's one-channel semiring sufficed for it.

8.2 · Negotiation — the trust model moves inside the algebra

Negotiation is where Rev 1 drew the line: a promise that is binding only if kept is not a verifiable claim about the present, and modelling whether to trust it (or whether to lie) was said to "leave the clean semiring" for subjective logic [8] over a reputation state, with action choice handed to game theory. The revision's central payoff is that the first half of that hand-off is now internal.

Subjective logic is the graded bilattice. A subjective-logic opinion is a triple (belief, disbelief, uncertainty). Read it as a graded Belnap value: belief is the for-channel \(a^{+}\), disbelief is the against-channel \(a^{-}\), uncertainty mass measures gap-ness (proximity to \(\mathbf{N}\)), and conflict mass measures glut-ness (proximity to \(\mathbf{B}\)) — the \([0,1]^2\) instance of §4.2. So Rev 1 did not actually leave the algebra when it reached for subjective logic; it reached for the algebra's own graded form without recognizing it. The reputation state and the claim grade are the same kind of object.

Two consequences follow, and both are the kind of thing Rev 1 could not say:

A broken promise is a glut. When a power promises to support your move, that pledge enters the for-channel of a claim about their cooperation. When the adjudicator later reveals they did the opposite, the betrayal enters the against-channel. Corroborating the two with \(\oplus_k\) climbs to \((\top,\top)=\mathbf{B}\): you were told both — they said they would and they demonstrably did not. A broken promise is not a gap in your knowledge of them; it is a glut, and the algebra now records it as one.

Reputation update is motion in the knowledge order. Each adjudicated round pours evidence into one channel or the other, moving a power's standing up \(\le_k\) — toward \(\mathbf{T}\) (reliably keeps promises), toward \(\mathbf{F}\) (reliably defects), or toward \(\mathbf{B}\) (says one thing, does another). A "known double-crosser" is simply a power whose accumulated con-mass and glut history dominate — a value in the algebra, not an annotation beside it.

8.3 · Where the boundary now sits

The boundary moves inward, but it does not vanish. What is now native: the representation of trust — a power's reliability, the distinction between "no read on them yet" (gap) and "caught them lying" (glut), and the accumulation of evidence as the game proceeds — all live in the algebra as graded Belnap values. What remains outside: the selection of action — whether to keep your own promise, whom to deceive, when to defect — is a strategic choice under incentives, and that is game theory and opponent modelling (the order of work CICERO operationalized [22, 23]), not algebra. A useful way to state CICERO-style honesty in these terms: keep your own for-channel consistent with your adjudicated actions, so that no glut ever accrues against you. The algebra can hold and propagate the trust state; it cannot tell you whether to be trustworthy.

9 · Where the algebra stops rev

A claim algebra makes the structural failure modes — silent error propagation, missing-value laundering, untracked provenance, and now contradiction-masquerading-as-ignorance — into type errors and algebraic impossibilities. It does not make the statistical and strategic problems go away. The revision removes one limitation Rev 1 listed and adds three costs it created; both honesties belong here.

Resolved: conflict and non-monotonic retraction. Rev 1 listed this as a limitation — "a contradiction collapses to the same \(\bot\) as the unknown; representing it needs a bilattice [7] or a semiring with monus." That is the work this revision does. Gap (\(\mathbf{N}\)) and glut (\(\mathbf{B}\)) are now distinct values; negation and the con-channel express refutation; and a superseding amendment is a retraction (§6) rather than a silent overwrite. The positive/monotone restriction is lifted. It is lifted at a price, stated next.

The three new costs of going bilattice.

The limitations that still stand.

Correlation blindness — now sharper. The homomorphic story assumes the leaves are independent; the moment two sources draw on the same upstream document, multiplying their confidences double-counts. The bilattice makes this worse to ignore, not better: a glut computed from a for-source and an against-source that secretly share a cause is a spurious contradiction, and an apparent corroboration may be one document echoing itself. Faithful handling needs the provenance to carry the correlation structure and the valuation to live in a domain that respects it (a measure or copula), which is exactly the \(\#\mathrm{P}\)-hard regime above.

Calibration is exogenous. The algebra propagates confidence soundly given the leaf numbers; it does not produce them. Whether "High" on an extraction corresponds to anything in the world is an empirical calibration problem, upstream of the algebra and unchanged by it.

Semantic correctness is not verification — and verification is not Belnap's axis. verify() checks that a value faithfully reproduces its cited provenance, not that the value is true: a faithful citation of a wrong source passes. And, as §4.4 insists, verification is a third axis — does-it-reproduce-the-source — distinct from Belnap's evidential axis of told-true/told-false content. Conflating the two (Rev 1's loose move) is the one error this revision most wants not to repeat one level up.

Time, retraction, and aggregates. The structure is still most natural for a single timeless resolution over an acyclic provenance. Retraction is now partly handled — negation expresses supersession at a point in time — but genuinely temporal belief revision (incremental recomputation as evidence arrives and is withdrawn) and feedback cycles still need the iterative fixed-point machinery, and quantitative aggregates ("the average across these claims") still call for a semimodule structure over the bilattice [4] rather than the lattice operations alone.

Capacity contention (routing). In the cost instance, when routing decisions interact through shared, finite capacity the per-message shortest-path computation is no longer the whole story; the correct frame is min-cost multi-commodity flow [21], which the algebra sets up but does not solve.

The contribution, stated precisely (revised). The mathematics is, as before, decades old — Belnap's four values (1977), Dunn's two-channel relational semantics, the Ginsberg–Fitting theory of bilattices and the twist representation, the Green–Karvounarakis–Tannen provenance semiring, and subjective logic. None of it is new. The claim is that the right wire format between LLM agents is a graded bilattice Claim — a two-channel, gap-distinct-from-glut, negation-closed value, fail-closed by the knowledge-meet annihilator, made trust-pluggable by a per-channel homomorphism and verifiable by an orthogonal predicate — and that committing to this object turns a list of correctness hopes into a list of theorems and type errors, with contradiction now among the things the type system can see rather than the things it silently drops.
