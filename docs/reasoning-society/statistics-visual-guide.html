The numbers behind the experiments

A visual guide to the statistics in the fallible-oracle program, written for a reader with freshman math and physics. Every chart below uses the real measured numbers from fallible-oracle-results.md.

The system we built plays a game: a society of small AI models tries to identify a hidden object, and a gate decides whether to officially sign an answer. Every game ends one of three ways, and the entire program is about the balance between them:

CORRECT
The gate signed the true answer. A win.
FAIL-OPEN
The gate signed a wrong answer. The one outcome the design exists to prevent — a confident lie.
ABSTAIN
The gate declined to sign. "I don't know," said honestly.

So every result you've seen is a count: out of N games, how many landed in each bucket. The statistics exist to answer one question about those counts: what do they let us honestly claim about the system's true behavior?

1. A count is not a rate — error bars for proportions

Suppose the gate signed a wrong answer in 2 games out of 12. The observed rate is 2/12 ≈ 17%. But if you ran 12 more games, you would not get exactly 2 again — the same way 12 coin flips don't give exactly 6 heads. The honest statement is a confidence interval: the range of true rates that could plausibly have produced what you saw.

We use the Wilson interval throughout. You don't need to derive it — you need to read it. Here are the program's headline results drawn as intervals:

Dots are the observed rate; bars are the range of true rates the data can't rule out. Two lessons jump out. First, small N means wide bars: 2/12 looks like "17%" but is really "anywhere from 5% to 45%." Second, zero observed is not zero proven — 0/52 still leaves room for a true rate up to 6.9%. The green results are the seam-closure wins: their whole bar sits below the red results' dots, which is what "the fix worked" means statistically.
Physics translation. This is the same discipline as a lab measurement: never report a value without its uncertainty, and never call two values "different" unless their error bars separate. A proportion from N games has uncertainty that shrinks like 1/√N — the same law as the standard error of a mean.
The Wilson formula, for the curious

For x successes in n trials with z = 1.96 (95%):

( p̂ + z²/2n  ±  z·√( p̂(1−p̂)/n + z²/4n² ) ) / (1 + z²/n)

where p̂ = x/n. It behaves sensibly at the edges (0 or n successes), which the naive p̂ ± z√(p̂q̂/n) does not. Handy special case used constantly in our results: for zero events, the 95% upper bound is z²/(n+z²) ≈ 3.84/(n+3.84). A rougher classroom version is the rule of three: see 0 events in n trials, and the true rate is very likely below 3/n.

2. Why "we saw zero" means more at N=64 than at N=16

This one decided a whole experiment. Early on, the all-weak society signed nothing in 16 games at a degraded oracle, and the temptation was to declare "it can't sign at all — any sign from a stronger model proves improvement." The committee refused, and this curve is why:

The curve is the 95% upper bound on the true rate after observing zero events in N games — roughly 3.84/(N+3.84). At N=16 a true rate of 19% is still possible, so "zero" is compatible with the very effect we were testing for. At N=64, zero pushes the bound to 5.7%, below the whole hypothesized band. Same observation ("nothing happened"), completely different evidential weight. That is why the stronger-closer trial was sized at 64 games per arm — and why its final "0/64 at both tiers" is a genuine finding, not an absence.

3. Telling two rates apart — what "powered" means

The trial's real question was a comparison: does the strong arm sign correctly more often than the weak arm? Each arm gives you a measured rate with a blur around it, and you can only claim a difference when the blurs separate. More games shrink the blur like 1/√N:

Hypothetical arms with true rates 6% and 20%, shown as dot ± 2 standard errors. At N=16 per arm the intervals overlap almost entirely; even a real effect would be unreadable. At N=64 they separate. "Powered" just means: N was chosen in advance so that, if the effect is real at the size we hypothesized, the bars will separate about 80% of the time. (The pre-registered method for the difference interval was Newcombe's, a Wilson-based version of exactly this picture.)
What actually happened: at N=64 the strong arms came in at literally 0 — so instead of separating upward, they proved the hypothesized 15–25% effect absent (section 2's logic). A powered design gives you a real answer either way; an underpowered one gives you noise either way.

4. Why independent checks multiply safety — and correlated ones don't

The redundancy experiment asked: if one confirmation of a guess can be wrong 30% of the time, does demanding k confirmations help? If the confirmations fail independently, errors multiply: 0.30, then 0.30² = 0.09, then 0.30³ = 0.027. If they fail together (correlation ρ = 1), asking again is asking the same broken instrument twice:

Measured points (800 hermetic games per cell) against the independent-failure theory. Along ρ=0 the data lands on (1−p)k almost exactly — 0.09 predicted, 0.09 measured at k=2. Along ρ=1 the curve is flat at the single-check error. The middle curve is the dangerous real world: partial correlation quietly eats most of the benefit.
Physics translation. This is common-mode noise. Two voltmeters sharing a noisy ground don't average their noise away, because it isn't independent between them — the √N improvement you learned for repeated measurements assumes independent errors. Redundant checks are repeated measurements of "is this answer right," and the same assumption is doing all the work.

5. Measuring the correlation between two graders — ρ

The curve above dialed ρ. Experiment E3 measured it, by giving 24 deliberately hard yes/no questions to pairs of graders and counting where their errors overlap. Here's the same-model pair (two copies of the same AI), drawn question by question:

4/24 (16.7%). If their errors were independent, "both wrong" should hit about 1 square (0.21 × 0.21 × 24 ≈ 1.04).
Illustrative positions, real counts. Four shared misses where independence predicts one: the two graders aren't two opinions, they're mostly one opinion photocopied. Plugging the counts into the correlation formula gives ρ ≈ 0.75 for same-model pairs, ρ ≈ 0.51 across different AI families — and exactly 0 shared errors when one "grader" is a mechanical check, which never erred at all. That single column is the program's founding thesis as a number: only the non-generative check was independent.
The ρ formula, for the curious

With error rates qA, qB and joint error rate J (both wrong on the same question):

ρ = (J − q_A·q_B) / √( q_A(1−q_A) · q_B(1−q_B) )

ρ = 0 means J equals the independent product; ρ = 1 means they always err together (to the extent their rates allow). Same-model: (0.167 − 0.043)/0.165 ≈ 0.75. Caveat we carry honestly: N = 24, so these are order-of-magnitude estimates; the ordering (same > cross-family > mechanical) is the result, not the second decimal.

6. The physics of asking noisy questions — channel capacity

The deepest result may be the simplest to state. The game is literally Twenty Questions, and the classic fact about that game is: 20 reliable yes/no answers carry 20 bits, enough to single out one object among 220 ≈ a million. But our degraded oracle lies 30% of the time — and Shannon's information theory prices exactly how much each noisy answer is still worth:

A coin-flip oracle (p = 0.5) tells you nothing — capacity 0. A perfect one carries a full bit. The curve between is steep in a way intuition misses: 70% reliable sounds mostly-trustworthy, but is worth barely a tenth of a bit per answer.
At p = 0.7, sixteen noisy answers carry about 1.9 bits — enough to tell apart roughly four candidates; at p = 0.9, about 8.5 bits (~360); at a perfect oracle, the full 16 (65,536). Whether that is enough depends entirely on how many candidates the hidden object could be.

The committee reported back — and the update is the best lesson in this guide. The arithmetic above survived review untouched: every capacity number was reproduced, and a stronger reasoner genuinely cannot squeeze extra bits from a noisy channel (with feedback, Shannon proved, you can reach capacity but never beat it). The conclusion did not survive. The ceiling argument compared the channel's ~2 bits to the wrong quantity — the ~10-bit space of "almost anything," which is the space the society had to search only because the rules withheld the candidate list. The actual answer was always one of eight words: 3.0 bits. Against the true 3-bit task, 1.9 bits does not forbid winning. So the null had two causes stacked: a real channel limit (why upgrading the model changed nothing — every tier faces the same noisy answers), and a search burden the protocol itself imposed (why even a perfect oracle only reached ~12% wins). The physics was real; we had blamed it for a problem the rulebook was causing.

The experiment that settled it did not wait for p = 0.9. If the search burden was the binding constraint, then telling the society the eight candidates — changing no model, no oracle, nothing else — should move wins off zero. It did: set withheld, 0/64; set revealed, 5/62 ≈ 8%, difference interval [0.008, 0.175], excluding zero. And the rate at which wrong guesses reached the oracle stayed identical (w = 0.016 in both arms): the sentence did not make the society guess more, it helped it find the right candidate. The corrected accounting even predicts the original mystery: with the set withheld, expected wins in 64 games ≈ 0.23, so the chance of seeing exactly zero ≈ 0.79. The zero we spent a week explaining was the corrected theory's expected outcome.

Same model, same lying oracle (p = 0.7). One sentence — the eight candidates — moved wins off an exact zero. Difference interval [0.008, 0.175]; wrong-guess rate unchanged at 0.016 in both arms.
Physics translation. H(ε) = −ε log₂ε − (1−ε)log₂(1−ε) is the same entropy function as in statistical mechanics, here measuring the uncertainty a 30%-lying answer leaves behind. Capacity C = 1 − H(ε) is the residue of useful information. And "no reasoner beats capacity" is the information-theoretic cousin of "no engine beats Carnot" — still true, for the channel. Our error was applying the bound to the wrong reservoir: we priced the search space the rules created, not the answer space that existed. The bound was physics; the search burden was a choice, and choices can be changed with a sentence.

7. The composed cell in one picture

Finally, the experiment that closed the architecture question. Same improved society, same targets, two arms differing in exactly one thing: whether two agents agreeing could sign on their own (seam open) or every signature had to pass the ground-truth check (seam gated):

correct fail-open (a signed lie) abstain
All 15 lies in the open arms came through the two-agents-agree path; the gate that requires ground truth eliminates every one (0/52, bar sits at [0, 6.9%] from section 1). The wins did not just survive — the per-game logs show 5 of the 6 gated wins are resurrected games: games that would have ended in a signed lie kept playing under the gate, and won. Two of them (spoon, pencil) are literally the same game that lied in the open arm converted into a correct signature in the gated one. Closing the lying path was not a tax on winning; it was a small subsidy.

8. The rules that made the numbers trustworthy

Three habits show up in every experiment above, and they're worth naming because they are what separates these charts from wishful reading:

Pre-registration — call the shot before shooting. Success thresholds, the interval method, sample sizes, and even the "if the result is ambiguous, we top up to N=150" rule were written and committed before each run. That's why a null result counts as a finding: the target was painted before the arrow flew.

Within-run controls — compare under one stamp. The composed cell reran its own control arm in the same invocation rather than comparing against a months-old run, and every run prints a configuration stamp (a hash of the prompts, budgets, and graders). Comparing across different stamps is comparing different experiments; the stamp makes that mistake visible.

Declared deviations and superseded claims stay on the record. When a measurement refuted a pre-registered expectation (gated wins were predicted ≤ open wins; they rose), the old claim was annotated as superseded — not silently edited. The document keeps its own history the way the ledger keeps the system's. The capacity ceiling in §6 is this rule in action: promised to committee, arithmetic kept, conclusion refuted, refutation published in the same file as the theory.

The one-paragraph summary of everything above. Counts come with Wilson error bars, and zero-with-large-N is evidence while zero-with-small-N is nothing (§1–2). Comparisons need enough games for the bars to separate (§3). Redundant checks help exactly as much as they're independent (§4), and we measured that AI checkers — even different brands — are far from independent, while a mechanical check was perfectly so (§5). A noisy oracle has a real channel ceiling, but that is only half the story: the p = 0.7 null turned out to be decoder-bound — revealing the hidden set moved the win rate off zero while a stronger model did not (§6). And with all of that in place, closing the one unchecked signing path eliminated every lie without costing a single win (§7) — provable because the rules in §8 were followed.
