The Diplomacy program

Motivated deception meets the ledger — a brief for the next measurement program.

Prepared for the operator · 2026-07-10 · status: FOR REVIEW — this document is itself the enflame test

1 · The question the last program could not ask

The fallible-oracle program tested trust against random unreliability. The oracle at p = 0.7 lied thirty percent of the time with no memory, no agenda, and no idea what you believed — the capacity arithmetic required that independence. Against that adversary, the results are now published: agreement between models is not verification; the gate that refuses to sign testimony costs nothing and keeps questions alive; model tier cannot buy soundness; task information can. Every one of those findings has a sequel question in a world the last apparatus could not build: what survives when the liar is motivated? When the false claim is crafted to be believed, timed to your commitments, and adapted to your defenses — does testimony-never-signs still hold the line, or does a clever adversary route around a ledger the way a random one never could?

There is also a foil worth naming. Meta's Cicero (2022) played Diplomacy at a human level; its designers aimed for honest play, and subsequent analyses documented it deceiving anyway — an agent optimized to win whose honesty was asserted rather than auditable. The inversion is the interesting experiment: an agent whose honesty is auditable by construction — every promise a ledger entry, every entry discharged or refuted by the board — with win rate left as the open empirical question. Not "can an AI win at Diplomacy" (answered) but "what does a receipts-carrying agent's presence do to a table of liars" (not answered by anyone).

2 · Why Diplomacy is the smallest world with the whole problem

Your own design documents found the reason two months ago, and it deserves to be quoted as the program's spine: "two regimes meet at adjudication." Everything an agent says — every alliance, threat, and promise — is unverifiable testimony at the moment it is said. Everything an agent does is resolved by a pure, deterministic adjudicator that no one can argue with. Diplomacy is a world where words carry zero mechanical trust and deeds carry perfect mechanical trust, with nothing in between — which is the claim algebra's ontology, implemented as a board game in 1959. The betrayal incentive is not a bug to control for; it is the load the apparatus exists to apply.

4 · The genuinely new part — the commitment calculus

The theorem-shaped core, and the reason this program extends the mathematics rather than merely applying it. The existing calculus handles claims about the present — asserted, then discharged or refuted by a check that runs now. A promise is a claim about the future: it cannot be checked at assertion time even in principle, which is a different lifecycle, not a slower one. The extension:

A commitment is not a new algebraic object — it is a new kind of claim, on the exact precedent testimony set: same carrier, same statuses, same fold and bilattice values, a different table of legal moves. Commit(agent, order-pattern, horizon) — "my Spring orders will include support A → Gal" — embeds in Claim by a forgetful map (drop the horizon and counterparty), so all eight proved results transfer by inheritance rather than re-proof. Pre-horizon it sits at gap (N, told-nothing-checkable), exactly like any claim on a lazy check — the horizon is not a new semantics, it is the earliest run-time of the check, and lazy discharge was already the house default. (The Belnap values are told-values, which is why Aristotle's sea-battle never arises: the ledger never asserted present truth for anything, only evidence state.) At the horizon the adjudicator discharges mechanically: orders match the pattern (kept → signed) or diverge (broken → refuted). No judgment anywhere in the resolution path. Renegotiation is supersession, never edit — a revised deal points at the one it replaces, must name the same counterparty, and takes effect only on that counterparty's acceptance; the old entry then retires to trace. So a stab cannot be laundered as an amendment — not by editing, and not by retiring a promise made to one power through a deal a different power accepts.

What the kind's law table adds — four rules plain assertions do not carry, and the extension's true proof burden: truthmaker control (only the agent who controls the future fact can commit to it — the identical proposition from anyone else is a prediction, ordinary testimony, no horizon discharge, no reputation stake; the "my" is kind-determining); consent-gated supersession (post-acceptance, no unilateral withdrawal — the plain-claim calculus proves idempotent absorption of withdrawal, and the commitment kind removes that operation, so the one non-inherited obligation is showing the eight results survive the restriction); monotone resolution (N → {signed, refuted, retired}, one-way at horizon — the new lemma); and gap-at-horizon for unmet guards (VOID, per §9). Two new lemmas, one restriction-preservation check, zero re-proofs.

Resolution under game-end and elimination is deliberately blunt in v1 — untested promises void with recorded reasons, never resolve — trading a known, expensive, instrumented exclusion for determinism; the causation-based v2 design is preserved in the committee record with an empirical revival trigger.

The laws to prove — the part built for the operator whose favorite phase is making the theorems work:

  • Fold immutability under time: the ledger fold over a game's event sequence is insensitive to when you look — no transition ever rewrites a resolved commitment (the calculus's strong normalization, extended over horizons; the extension must preserve all eight proved results).
  • Monotone resolution: ACTIVE → {KEPT, BROKEN, SUPERSEDED} is one-way; nothing un-breaks. Retro-signing is unrepresentable, not merely forbidden.
  • Conditional commitments as guarded implication: "I support you into Gal if you move there" discharges only when the guard is observed at the same horizon; an unmet guard resolves the whole commitment VOID, never BROKEN — you cannot be a traitor to a deal whose condition never fired. Formally VOID is not a new value: it is the gap (Belnap's N) arriving at a horizon — the claim was never checkable — and its ledger disposition is retirement with a guard-unmet trace reason, not refutation.
  • Reputation as a derived statistic, with its two faces reconciled: inside the algebra, trust stays the operator's design — the subjective-logic opinion ω = (b, d, u, a) as the graded bilattice's [0,1]² instance, updated from resolutions. In the reporting layer, the same counts yield kept/resolved with a Wilson interval — reputation with a confidence bound, queryable per agent, per game, per opponent. One count, two projections; prove they agree on ordering.
  • The inherited asterisk: the calculus itself declares absolute soundness false by design, “its violation rate an experimental quantity” — the fallible-oracle cells measured that rate under a noisy checker; the D-cells below measure it under a motivated one. The commitment extension does not soften the asterisk; it takes it to a harder table.
  • Glut under intent: two allies asserting contradictory board-adjacent claims is no longer noise — it is a strategy. The glut value (Belnap's B, reached under ⊗ₖ when support and refutation meet) and the harness's contested warning — a live claim with a refutation about it and no successor or retirement — get their first adversarial stress test.

Lineage note. The logic of agents binding themselves to actions in a branching future has a canonical formalization — stit theory ("sees to it that") — and its principal architect is Nuel Belnap, the same logician whose four values ground this algebra; Horty's deontic layer treats obligations as stit-constraints. The commitment kind reunites the two halves of one man's work: Belnap's evidence values holding the ledger, Belnap's agency logic shaping the kind's law table. We take the vocabulary and the guardrails, not the full branching-time machinery — the do-not list applies to metaphysics too.

5 · The apparatus — endorse the milestones, shrink the world

The implementation spec's M0–M4 ladder stands (adjudicator first, no LLM in sight; scripted agents; then LLM orders; then negotiation; then full games). Two amendments, both applications of the last program's hardest-won lesson — the smallest world that contains the whole problem:

Amendment 1 — Trio before the standard map. The spec already licenses a small-map variant before the seventeen-province nuances; the brief makes it concrete. Trio: three powers, thirteen provinces, six supply centers, armies only — no fleets, no coasts, no convoys. Victory at four centers. Every essential mechanic survives: a 2-against-1 attack needs a support order (the commitment unit), any two powers can squeeze the third (the alliance incentive), and the supporter can always stab (the betrayal payoff). What the cut buys: the M0 adjudicator shrinks from the full DATC gauntlet to the movement-and-support core (DATC 6.A basics plus 6.D support-cutting) — days, not weeks — and a full game costs a handful of LLM turns instead of dozens, which is what makes N = 50-per-arm cells affordable. The standard map is the program's later expansion, not its entry fee.

Amendment 2 — instrument from the first commit. The spec's Chronicle becomes the Archiver pattern from day one: every game a stamped record with a header line the digest tooling can distill, so the verify-results suite covers Diplomacy tables the week they exist. The experiments are not something added after M4; the M-ladder builds the apparatus and the D-ladder below is the reason it exists. Commitment resolution enters the ledger through the existing machinery, not around it: a new check family (say check: "trio-adjudicator") that the game's gate step signs on resolution, mirroring ledger/gate.py's family sweep — zero new signing paths, one new family name.

Pre-registered predictions (mine, stated before any apparatus exists, so they can die properly):

P1 — D1: reputation will predict survival, and the effect will be larger than most game-theory priors expect, because LLM agents (unlike Nash agents) read and react to stated histories.

P2 — D2: the receipts agent will be preferred as an ally (higher alliance formation), and will suffer fewer betrayals, not more — receipts read as predictability, and predictable partners are valuable even to the selfish. Directional, falsifiable, and I note the respectable opposite prior: legible honesty paints a target.

P3 — D3: deceiver tier will matter more than closer tier did in the last program (crafted lies are not channel noise) — but the commitment-tracking defense will flatten the tier gradient, because past resolutions are the one input the deceiver's eloquence cannot rewrite.

P4 — somewhere in D1–D3, an agent will attempt to launder a broken commitment through renegotiation language after the horizon. The supersession-not-edit law exists for that moment; I predict we will watch it fire.

Cost honesty. A Trio game is roughly 6–10 negotiation-and-orders phases × 3 powers; at small-model rates that is cents per game and D-cells at N = 50–100 per arm are afternoon-scale runs, which is precisely what the map shrink purchases. Center counts and survival give continuous-ish outcomes, so the ladder is not condemned to the 0/64 power problem that haunted the win-rate-only cells. The standard map at seven powers is 10–20× the cost per game and joins the program only when a Trio result earns the replication. One honest limit rides on the three-power cut: a truel biases toward caution and makes the 2-against-1 squeeze the dominant pattern, so D1's honesty economics and D2's receipts effects are measured under truel structure — whether they generalize to N-power tables is exactly what the standard-map expansion tests, and D1's pre-registration will carry this threat to external validity verbatim.

The commitment calculus is the deliverable; Diplomacy is the forcing function that makes it honest.

8 · The do-not list, day one

  • No standard 7-power map in v1 — Trio until a result earns the expansion.
  • No fleets, coasts, or convoys in v1 — the adjudicator's hard 20% serves no D-cell.
  • No RL, no self-play training, no rating-ladder chase — this is a measurement program, not an agent olympics.
  • No graphical board beyond a debug SVG — the Chronicle is the interface of record.
  • No open-world negotiation (the bottom-right of Figure 1) — that jurisdiction has no adjudicator, and going there without one would be selling certification where no check exists.

9 · Open decisions for the operator

  • The map: Trio as sketched (13/6/3, armies only, solo at 4), or a different minimal geometry. The adjacency shape matters more than the names.
  • Ladder scope: D0–D3 as the funded program with D4 as stretch (recommended), or trim further.
  • VOID as gap-at-horizon: confirm the identification — an unmet guard resolves to Belnap's N at the horizon and retires (never refutes) — or argue for a distinct terminal value. A calculus decision, not an implementation detail.
  • Reporting split: subjective-logic ω inside the algebra, Wilson interval in the reports, agreement-on-ordering as a proved lemma — confirm or amend.
  • The name. "The Diplomacy program" is serviceable; the repo directory wants something shorter. Trio itself is available.

The last stalking horse was a children's guessing game that ended by refuting a misapplication of Shannon in public, with receipts. This one is a board game about 1914 — a year already sitting in your repo as a claim ledger — and it ends, if it ends anywhere, at the only JARVIS question left standing: not can the assistant be trusted when the world is noisy; that is published. Can it be trusted when the world wants something from it — and can it be the one at the table whose word, uniquely, comes with receipts.
