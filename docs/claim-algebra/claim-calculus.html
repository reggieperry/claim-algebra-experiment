A Claim Calculus:
the operational meta-theory of the claim algebra

A working note · claim-algebra-lab · docs/claim-algebra/

Abstract. The claim algebra equips evidential claims with combining operations and equational laws. This note develops the companion calculus: a small reduction system whose terms are sequences of evidence events on one claim slot, whose normal forms are resolved beliefs, and whose acceptance judgment is the signing gate. We define the system from primitives, then prove eight results — determinacy, strong normalization, confluence on the commutative fragment, idempotent absorption of withdrawal, non-fabrication, exclusivity, fail-closed propagation across a derivation network, and relative soundness — and we state precisely the two propositions that are not theorems: confluence of the full reduction (false by design) and absolute soundness (false, and its violation rate is the experiment's headline metric). Every symbol is defined before use; a glossary of notation closes the note.

1.Introduction

An algebra is a set of values closed under operations that satisfy equational laws; reasoning in an algebra is reasoning by equality between terms. A calculus, in the sense used here, is a deductive or operational system: a syntax of terms, a relation of reduction (a directed, step-by-step rewriting), and a judgment that classifies terms. Reasoning in a calculus is reasoning by derivation. The two are complementary: a calculus is studied for its meta-theory (does reduction terminate? is it order-independent? does it preserve the judgment?), and it is related to an algebra by an adequacy result stating that the operational and equational views agree.

The claim algebra of this project ([6], [7]) gives evidential claims a combining structure: a carrier of testimonies over a free provenance semiring, four-valued (Belnap [1]) corners read structurally from the provenance, and a signing gate. Much of a calculus is already latent in it — the event fold of the workbench is a reduction; the provenance polynomial is a derivation witness in the sense of provenance semirings ([3]); the gate is an acceptance judgment. This note makes that calculus explicit and proves what can be proved.

Contributions. (i) A reduction system over evidence events with the resolved belief as its normal form (§3). (ii) Eight proved meta-theorems (§§4–7), including fail-closed propagation across the derivation network (Theorem 6.7). (iii) An honest delimitation of the two non-theorems and an account of why they fail (§§5, 7, 9). (iv) An adequacy proposition relating the calculus to the algebra (§8). The load-bearing lemmas — that corroboration is a commutative monoid, and that striking is idempotent — are mechanically checked in the project's test suite, so the results that reduce to them inherit that backing; the remainder are paper proofs over the operations' definitions.

2.Preliminaries: the claim algebra

We recall the algebra, defining each notion before it is used. The reader familiar with [7] may skim; the calculus of §3 depends only on the definitions collected here.

Definition 2.1 (lineage). A lineage is an atomic citation token, drawn from a fixed countable set X. Each lineage names one piece of grounded evidence — a cited source span.
Definition 2.2 (semiring; provenance). A commutative semiring is a set with two associative, commutative operations — addition + with identity 0, and multiplication · with identity 1 — in which · distributes over + and 0 annihilates (0 · x = 0). A provenance is an element of the free commutative semiring ℕ[X]: a polynomial with non-negative integer coefficients over monomials in the lineages X. Intuitively + is alternative derivation (corroboration), · is joint use (conjunction), 0 is the absence of support, and 1 is a derivation that cites nothing specific. We write isZero(p) for the predicate p = 0. A key property used below: ℕ has no additive inverses, so p + q = 0 implies p = q = 0 (addition is zero-sum-free; support, once present, is never subtracted away).
Definition 2.3 (graded pair; channels). A claim carries two provenance channels — the paired representation of the bilattice's two orders [2]: pro (support for a value) and con (support against it). A graded pair is an element (p, c) ∈ ℕ[X] × ℕ[X].
Definition 2.4 (testimony; candidate carrier). Fix a set A of values. A testimony over A is a finite partial map t : A ⇀ ℕ[X] × ℕ[X] assigning each candidate value its graded pair, normalized so that no entry is (0, 0). We write cand(t) for the set of values on which t is defined, and pro(t, a), con(t, a) for the two components of t(a) (both 0 when a ∉ cand(t)).
Definition 2.5 (channel totals; the gap). The channel totals of a testimony are provPro(t) = Σa∈cand(t) pro(t, a) and provCon(t) = Σa∈cand(t) con(t, a), summed in ℕ[X]. The gap, written ε, is the empty testimony (cand(ε) = ∅, so both totals are 0).
Definition 2.6 (Belnap value; corner). The four Belnap values are N (gap, "told nothing"), T (true, "told for"), F (false, "told against"), and B (glut, "told both"). The corner of a testimony is the structural read 
corner(t) = β( isZero(provPro(t)), isZero(provCon(t)) ),
where  β(true,  true ) = N    β(false, true ) = T
       β(true,  false) = F    β(false, false) = B.
The corner depends only on whether each channel carries support, never on a numeric grade — it is model-free.
Definition 2.7 (cardinality). The cardinality of a testimony is the number of pro-supported candidates, card(t) = #{ a ∈ cand(t) : pro(t, a) ≠ 0 }, where #(·) is the size of a finite set.
Definition 2.8 (signable value). The signable value value(t) is the unique a with pro(t, a) ≠ 0 when card(t) = 1 and provCon(t) = 0 (a clean, unambiguous T); otherwise it is undefined.
Definition 2.9 (corroborate, ⊕). The corroboration of testimonies a, b is the testimony a ⊕ b with, for every v ∈ cand(a) ∪ cand(b), (a ⊕ b)(v) = ( pro(a,v) + pro(b,v), con(a,v) + con(b,v) ), normalized. It pools channels pointwise per candidate and keeps disagreeing values as distinct candidates.
Proposition 2.10 (corroboration is a commutative monoid). (⊕, ε) is associative and commutative with two-sided identity ε.
Proof. Immediate from Definition 2.9: each property reduces, channel- and candidate-wise, to the corresponding property of + in ℕ[X] (a commutative monoid with identity 0), and the normalization in Definition 2.4 removes only (0,0) entries, which + never reintroduces. The commutative-monoid laws of + in ℕ[X] are themselves machine-checked (RingLaws[Prov].commutativeRig), and ⊕ inherits them by this pointwise reduction, which is property-tested on Testimony. ∎
Definition 2.11 (refute, ¬). The refutation ¬t swaps the two channels of every candidate: (¬t)(v) = ( con(t,v), pro(t,v) ). It is involutive: ¬¬t = t.
Definition 2.12 (strike). The strike of a testimony clears the pro-channel and folds all support onto con: (strike t)(v) = ( 0, pro(t,v) + con(t,v) ). It models a deletion that retains the struck value on record for audit.
Proposition 2.13 (strike is idempotent). strike(strike t) = strike t.
Proof. For each v, the first strike yields (0, p + c) where p = pro(t,v), c = con(t,v); the second yields (0, 0 + (p + c)) = (0, p + c). The two agree. (Idempotence needs only that 0 is the additive identity; the con total p + c is unchanged by the second strike.) This proposition is property-tested in the suite. ∎
Definition 2.14 (supersession). A supersession is a pair s = (struck(s), operative(s)) of testimonies. The constructor supersede(t, g) = (¬t, g) strikes the prior t to the con-channel (via ¬) and installs the amendment g as the operative claim. Keeping the two as a pair — rather than corroborating them — is what prevents a struck prior plus a fresh operative from reading as a glut (cf. Theorem 6.3).
Definition 2.15 (trust model; the gate; acceptance). A trust model is a semiring homomorphism ν̂ : ℕ[X] → M into a totally ordered commutative semiring M (the render carrier); the grade of a testimony is ν̂(provPro(t)). Given a threshold θ ∈ M and a verifier predicate verify on testimonies, the gate accepts t, written Accept(t) = value(t), exactly when 
corner(t) = T   ∧   card(t) = 1   ∧   ν̂(provPro(t)) ≥ θ   ∧   verify(t);
otherwise the gate blocks. A blocked gate carries no value. We say the gate signs v when Accept(t) = v.

3.The claim calculus

We now define the calculus proper: its terms, its reduction relation, and its judgment. A slot is one (deal, concept) position whose evidence accumulates over time; the calculus describes one slot.

Definition 3.1 (event). An event is one of three forms: Assert(g) and Supersede(g), each carrying a testimony g (the grounded contribution), or Withdraw (a parameterless deletion).
Definition 3.2 (term; event sequence). A term is a finite sequence E = [e1, …, en] of events, ordered by arrival. We write |E| = n for its length, [] for the empty term, and E ⧺ [e] for E extended by one event.
Definition 3.3 (belief). A belief is one of two forms: Open(t), a plain testimony, or Repl(s), a supersession pair (Definition 2.14). The initial belief is b0 = Open(ε).
Definition 3.4 (one-step reduction, ⟨b, e⟩ ⤳ b′). The reduction of a belief by an event is given by exhaustive case analysis: 
⟨Open(t), Assert(g)⟩     ⤳ Open(t ⊕ g)
⟨Open(t), Supersede(g)⟩  ⤳ Open(t)                    if corner(g) = N      (inert: empty amendment)
                          ⤳ Open(g)                    else if corner(t)=N  (fresh: nothing to supersede)
                          ⤳ Repl( supersede(t, g) )    otherwise
⟨Open(t), Withdraw⟩      ⤳ Open( strike t )
⟨Repl(s), Assert(g)⟩     ⤳ Repl( struck(s), operative(s) ⊕ g )
⟨Repl(s), Supersede(g)⟩  ⤳ Repl(s)                     if corner(g) = N
                          ⤳ Repl( supersede(operative(s), g) )   otherwise
⟨Repl(s), Withdraw⟩      ⤳ Repl( struck(s), strike(operative(s)) )
Definition 3.5 (evaluation, belief(E)). Evaluation runs the reduction left to right from the initial belief: belief([]) = b0, and belief(E ⧺ [e]) = b′ where ⟨belief(E), e⟩ ⤳ b′. The result is the term's normal form.
Definition 3.6 (resolution; signing). The status of a belief reads the gate: Status(Open(t)) = Accept(t) (Definition 2.15), and Status(Repl(s)) = Accept(operative(s)), retaining struck(s) only for the audit trace. A term E signs a value v when Status(belief(E)) = v (an acceptance); otherwise E is blocked. For brevity, "v originates in E" means some event of E is Assert(g) or Supersede(g) with pro(g, v) ≠ 0. Such an event contributes v to belief(E) when that pro-support survives, uncancelled, in the testimony the gate reads — the t of Open(t), or the operative(s) of Repl(s) — equivalently, when v is a pro-candidate there. A value later struck by a supersession (its support moved to the con-channel, Definition 2.14) does not contribute.

4.Operational meta-theory

Theorem 4.1 (determinacy). The one-step reduction ⤳ is a total function: for every belief b and event e there is exactly one b′ with ⟨b, e⟩ ⤳ b′.
Proof. The defining clauses of Definition 3.4 partition the cases by the form of b (two forms) and the form of e (three forms); within the Supersede clauses the side conditions corner(g) = N and corner(t) = N are decidable and ordered, so the alternatives are mutually exclusive and jointly exhaustive. Each right-hand side is a composition of total functions (⊕, supersede, strike) on finite maps. Hence ⤳ is single-valued and total. ∎
Theorem 4.2 (strong normalization). For every term E, evaluation halts, reaching belief(E) in exactly |E| reduction steps.
Proof. By induction on |E| using Definition 3.5. The base case |E| = 0 returns b0 in zero steps. For the inductive step, belief(E ⧺ [e]) performs the |E| steps of belief(E) (induction hypothesis) followed by one reduction (total, by Theorem 4.1), and each operation it invokes terminates on finite candidate maps. Thus |E| + 1 steps suffice and the result is well-defined. ∎

5.Confluence on the commutative fragment

Determinacy (Theorem 4.1) makes evaluation a function of the ordered term. The deeper question is when the result is independent of order — confluence, in the rewriting sense [4]. It is, exactly on the sub-language of assertions.

Definition 5.1 (assertion term). A term is an assertion term if every event in it has the form Assert(g).
Lemma 5.2 (permutation invariance of a commutative-monoid fold). Let (S, ⊕, ε) be a commutative monoid (carrier S distinct from the render carrier M of Definition 2.15) and x1, …, xn ∈ S. For every permutation π of {1, …, n}, ((ε ⊕ x1) ⊕ ⋯) ⊕ xn = ((ε ⊕ xπ(1)) ⊕ ⋯) ⊕ xπ(n).
Proof. By the identity law the leading ε may be dropped, leaving the product x1 ⊕ ⋯ ⊕ xn. Every permutation is a composition of adjacent transpositions, so it suffices to show invariance under one such transposition. Associativity rewrites a window of the product to isolate ⋯ ⊕ (xi ⊕ xi+1) ⊕ ⋯, and commutativity replaces xi ⊕ xi+1 by xi+1 ⊕ xi; associativity restores the original grouping. Induction on the number of transpositions in a factorization of π completes the argument. ∎
Theorem 5.3 (confluence on assertions). If E is an assertion term, then belief(E) depends only on the multiset of testimonies it asserts, not on their order. Equivalently, belief(E) = belief(E′) whenever E′ is a permutation of E.
Proof. Starting from b0 = Open(ε), the only applicable clause for an Assert event on an Open belief is ⟨Open(t), Assert(g)⟩ ⤳ Open(t ⊕ g), and its result is again Open, so the belief stays Open throughout. Hence for E = [Assert(g1), …, Assert(gn)], belief(E) = Open( ((ε ⊕ g1) ⊕ ⋯) ⊕ gn ). By Proposition 2.10 the operation (⊕, ε) is a commutative monoid, so Lemma 5.2 gives permutation invariance of the inner fold; Open(·) is injective. The claim follows. ∎

Theorem 5.3 is the order-independence guarantee an inter-agent system needs: the verdict on a slot cannot depend on the order in which independent agents corroborate it. We state the boundary of this guarantee explicitly.

Remark 5.4 (the full reduction is not confluent — by design). Outside the assertion fragment, evaluation is order-sensitive, and intentionally so. For grounded testimonies a ≠ b with disjoint single candidates, belief([Assert(a), Supersede(b)]) governs with b (the amendment supersedes), whereas belief([Supersede(b), Assert(a)]), with nothing yet to supersede, takes b as a fresh assertion and corroborates a into it, giving the single open belief Open(b ⊕ a) in which a and b are rival candidates. The two orders reach different verdicts — the first signs b, the second blocks as ambiguous. This is the correct semantics — a later amendment ought to override an earlier figure — and it is precisely why evaluation is a left fold rather than a multiset reduction. Confluence is therefore a theorem on the commutative sub-language and a (deliberate) non-theorem on the whole. The supersession and withdrawal fragment must instead be serialized per slot, a single-writer discipline; that requirement is the operational counterpart of Remark 5.4.

6.Safety: subject reduction

The safety meta-theorems state that reduction never manufactures an unsound acceptance: it cannot resurrect a struck value, cannot sign a value no event supplied, and cannot sign a contested one. Together they are the analogue of subject reduction (preservation) for this calculus.

Theorem 6.1 (idempotent absorption of withdrawal). Withdrawal is absorbing: for any belief b, reducing by Withdraw twice gives the same belief as reducing once. Consequently no number of withdrawals on a slot can restore a withdrawn value to signable status.
Proof. On an Open belief, ⟨Open(t), Withdraw⟩ ⤳ Open(strike t) and a second withdrawal yields Open(strike(strike t)), equal to Open(strike t) by Proposition 2.13. On a Repl belief the same applies to operative(s). After a strike, provPro = 0 (Definition 2.12), so corner ∈ {N, F} and the gate blocks (Definition 2.15); idempotence prevents a second withdrawal from changing this. ∎

Remark. Theorem 6.1 turns on withdrawal being modeled by the absorbing strike rather than the involutive ¬. A second strike re-clears an already-empty pro-channel and leaves the con total unchanged (Proposition 2.13, by the additive identity), so it is a no-op; a second refute, by contrast, would swap the channels back and resurrect the value. Modeling deletion as strike rather than ¬ is what makes the count of withdrawals irrelevant.

Theorem 6.2 (non-fabrication). If a term E signs a value v, then v originates in E (Definition 3.6): some asserting event supplied v with positive pro-support.
Proof. We first show, by induction on |E|, that the pro-support of the signable part of belief(E) — that is, of t when belief(E) = Open(t), or of operative(s) when belief(E) = Repl(s) — has every pro-candidate inherited from some event's pro-channel. The base belief b0 = Open(ε) has empty pro. For the step, inspect the clauses of Definition 3.4: Assert(g) applies ⊕, whose pro-support is the union of the two operands' pro-supports (Definition 2.9), adding only g's pro-candidates; the Supersede(g) clauses set the new signable testimony to g (fresh case) or to the operative g of supersede(t,g) (Definition 2.14), whose pro is g's; the inert case leaves the testimony unchanged; Withdraw applies strike (Definition 2.12), which only removes pro; and the ¬-struck prior of a supersession (Definition 2.14) lands on the con-channel, so it is never the signable testimony. No clause introduces a pro-candidate from nothing, establishing the invariant. Now if E signs v, then Accept(·) = v requires corner = T and value = v (Definitions 2.15, 2.8), so v has positive pro-support in the signable testimony; by the invariant it originates in some event. ∎
Theorem 6.3 (exclusivity). A term never signs a value carrying any con-support; in particular a glutted value (one asserted and refuted) is never signed.
Proof. Signing requires corner = T (Definition 2.15), i.e. isZero(provCon) — the con total over all candidates is 0 (Definition 2.6). By zero-sum-freeness (Definition 2.2) a sum in ℕ[X] is 0 only if every summand is 0, so no candidate carries con-support. A glut has corner = B ≠ T and is blocked outright. ∎

Remark 6.4. Theorem 6.3 explains the pairing in Definition 2.14: were the struck prior and the fresh operative corroborated into a single testimony, the prior's support would land on the con-channel, making provCon ≠ 0 and forcing corner = B, so a legitimate supersession would be misread as a glut and blocked. Representing supersession as the pair (¬t, g) keeps the operative's con empty, so the operative can sign. The exclusivity theorem and the pair representation are two views of one fact.

Composition across slots

The results so far concern one slot. We now add the composition by which the value at one slot is derived from the values at others — the conjunction nodes of the network, such as a leverage ratio derived from a debt and an EBITDA — and prove the fail-closed propagation that makes a single missing input block the entire derivation.

Definition 6.5 (derivation, ⊗k). For testimonies a over A and b over B and a total function f : A × B → C, the derivation a ⊗kf b — the knowledge-meet conjunction — is the testimony over C formed by joint use on both channels: for every pair (x, y) ∈ cand(a) × cand(b) it contributes to candidate f(x, y) the graded pair ( pro(a,x) · pro(b,y), con(a,x) · con(b,y) ), summing contributions that collide on one result value and normalizing (Definition 2.4). A derivation over an empty candidate set has no pairs to form.
Definition 6.6 (derivation term; its testimony). A derivation is a finite term D ::= leaf(E) | nodef(D1, D2), where E is an event term (Definition 3.2) standing at a leaf slot and nodef is a required conjunction. Let sig(b) denote the signable testimony of a belief: sig(Open(t)) = t and sig(Repl(s)) = operative(s). The testimony of a derivation, written ⟦D⟧, is ⟦leaf(E)⟧ = sig(belief(E)) and ⟦nodef(D1, D2)⟧ = ⟦D1⟧ ⊗kf ⟦D2⟧. A leaf is a gap when corner(sig(belief(E))) = N.
Theorem 6.7 (fail-closed propagation; no gap-laundering). If any leaf of a derivation D is a gap, then corner(⟦D⟧) = N at the root, and the gate blocks the root.
Proof. Two facts precede the induction. First, corner(t) = N iff t = ε: by Definition 2.6, N means provPro(t) = provCon(t) = 0; by zero-sum-freeness (Definition 2.2) a sum in ℕ[X] is 0 only if every summand is, so each candidate has both components 0, and normalization (Definition 2.4) deletes every (0,0) entry, leaving the empty testimony ε; conversely corner(ε) = N. Second, ε ⊗kf b = ε = a ⊗kf ε: since cand(ε) = ∅, the pair set of Definition 6.5 is empty and the result has no candidates. (This is the algebra's fail-closed annihilator N ⊗k x = N, mechanically checked as the gap-annihilation property of derive in the Testimony law suite — distinct from the render-carrier rig annihilator 0M · x = 0M.) Now induct on the structure of D. If D = leaf(E), the hypothesis gives corner(⟦D⟧) = N directly. If D = nodef(D1, D2) and the gap leaf lies in, say, D1, the induction hypothesis gives corner(⟦D1⟧) = N, so ⟦D1⟧ = ε, whence ⟦D⟧ = ε ⊗kf ⟦D2⟧ = ε and corner(⟦D⟧) = N. The gate (Definition 2.15) requires corner = T and therefore blocks. ∎

Remark 6.8. Theorem 6.7 is the formal content of fault F1 (a redacted leaf): a single missing required input cannot be laundered into a signature, however deep the derivation. It uses only the knowledge-meet ⊗k. An actively refuted input (corner F) is instead handled by the algebra's truth-meet conjunction — not formalized in this note — which carries F, not N, toward the root (the F7-versus-F1 distinction); either way the gate blocks it, by Theorem 6.3. This aside is informal.

7.Soundness

Soundness is the property that what the system signs is true. We must distinguish two readings, because one is provable and one is — necessarily — not. Fix a ground truth assignment that designates, for each slot, the correct value; call an asserting event faithful if the value it supplies on its pro-channel is the correct one.

Theorem 7.1 (relative soundness). If every event that contributes the signed value to belief(E) (Definition 3.6) is faithful, then the value E signs is correct. Contrapositively: if E signs an incorrect value, at least one contributing event was unfaithful.
Proof. Suppose E signs v. By Definition 2.15 the testimony the gate reads has corner = T and card = 1: exactly one pro-supported candidate, and (by Theorem 6.3) no con-support. By Definition 3.6 the contributing events are exactly those whose pro-support is live in that testimony. Were two contributing events to supply distinct values v1 ≠ v2, both would be live pro-candidates (Definition 2.9 keeps disagreeing values distinct), giving card ≥ 2; then value is undefined (Definition 2.8) and the gate blocks — contradicting that E signs. Hence every contributing event supplies the same value, the lone live pro-candidate v (which originates in E by Theorem 6.2); if each is faithful, v is correct. The argument is uniform over Open(t) and Repl(s), the gate-read testimony being t or operative(s) respectively. A value struck by a supersession is, by Definition 3.6, not contributing — its support having moved to the con-channel — so it is unconstrained by the hypothesis, and the theorem is silent on it (correctly: the struck value is not the one signed). ∎
Corollary 7.2 (conservativity). The calculus does not amplify the unfaithfulness of its inputs; it can only mask it by abstention. By Theorem 6.3 and the card = 1 requirement, any disagreement among contributing events (a conflict) or any refutation blocks rather than signs. Hence the set of slots the calculus signs is contained in the set on which the contributing events agree and ground, and its rate of signed-but-incorrect outcomes is at most the rate at which the inputs are simultaneously unfaithful and agreed. Inference reduces input error; it never manufactures it.
Non-theorem 7.3 (absolute soundness). The unconditional statement — if E signs v, then v is correct — is not a theorem, and is in general false. Its hypothesis quantifies over the faithfulness of the asserting events, which the calculus does not control: an asserting event is produced by an external oracle (a language model grounding a figure), and the oracle may ground a value that is plausible, cited, and verifiable against its own source yet incorrect. The gate then accepts it. The frequency of such signed-but-incorrect outcomes is the experiment's headline metric, the confidently-wrong-at-signature (CWS) rate. Theorem 7.1 localizes this failure precisely: it lies entirely in the events' faithfulness — the oracle — and not in the reduction. A calculus cannot prove the soundness of an oracle it does not govern; it can only, as Corollary 7.2 shows, refuse to compound the oracle's errors and abstain when its inputs conflict.

8.Adequacy

Finally we record that the operational system of §3 computes exactly the algebra's resolution, so the meta-theorems above are statements about the algebra, not about an unrelated model of it.

Proposition 8.1 (adequacy). For every term E, the normal form belief(E) of Definition 3.5 equals the algebraic fold of the events of E under the operations of §2, and Status(belief(E)) equals the gate decision the algebra assigns to that fold.
Proof. Definition 3.5 defines belief as the left fold of the step relation ⤳ from b0, and Definition 3.4 defines each step as the corresponding algebraic operation (⊕, supersede, strike) under the same side conditions the algebra uses (the corner tests of Definition 2.6). The two are therefore the same function by construction. Definition 3.6 reads Status through the same gate (Definition 2.15) the algebra signs by. Hence the operational and algebraic resolutions coincide. ∎

Adequacy is immediate here because the calculus was distilled from the algebra rather than posited independently; we state it so that the soundness, safety, and confluence results transfer without gap to the implemented system, whose evaluator is exactly this fold.

9.Discussion

The calculus is well defined and strongly normalizing (§4), confluent on the commutative fragment and deliberately order-sensitive beyond it (§5), and safe in the sense that reduction never fabricates, resurrects, or contests a signature, and a single missing required input cannot be laundered through derivation into one (§6). Its soundness is relative: faithful inputs yield correct signatures, and the calculus is conservative, abstaining under conflict rather than guessing (§7). The single property it cannot supply — that a signature is unconditionally correct — is the soundness of the external oracle, whose failure rate is exactly what the rig measures.
